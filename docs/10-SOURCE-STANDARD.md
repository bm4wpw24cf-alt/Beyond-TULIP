# 10. Source Standard

## Purpose

This document defines the standards for selecting, evaluating, and citing sources throughout the Beyond TULIP project.

Its purpose is to ensure that every article is based on reliable, verifiable, and appropriately weighted evidence.

---

## Primary Principle

Scripture is the primary source.

All other sources are secondary and should be evaluated in light of Scripture.

---

## Source Hierarchy

The AI should normally prioritize sources in the following order:

1. Biblical text
2. Original biblical languages
3. Historical context
4. Primary historical documents
5. Peer-reviewed academic scholarship
6. Standard reference works
7. Reputable commentaries
8. Credible educational resources

The authority of a source depends on both its reliability and its relevance.

---

## Primary Sources

Whenever possible, consult primary sources.

Examples include:

- The biblical text
- Early church writings
- Ecumenical councils
- Historic confessions
- Original theological works
- Ancient historical documents

Primary sources should be preferred over summaries of those sources.

---

## Secondary Sources

Secondary sources help explain primary sources.

Examples include:

- Academic commentaries
- Bible dictionaries
- Encyclopedias
- Scholarly monographs
- Journal articles

Secondary sources should support understanding rather than replace independent analysis.

---

## Evaluating Sources

The AI should evaluate every source according to:

- Accuracy
- Scholarly credibility
- Historical reliability
- Transparency
- Relevance
- Context

Popularity should never be confused with reliability.

---

## Multiple Perspectives

Whenever appropriate, consult scholars representing different theological traditions.

The AI should avoid relying exclusively on one tradition or one author.

---

## Quotations

Use quotations sparingly.

Quotes should:

- Preserve the author's intended meaning.
- Be accurately attributed.
- Support analysis rather than replace it.

Long quotations should normally be avoided.

---

## Citations

Every factual claim that depends upon an external source should be properly attributed.

Scripture references should always be clearly identified.

Historical claims should be supported by reliable historical evidence.

---

## Internet Sources

Online material should be evaluated carefully.

Preference should be given to:

- Academic institutions
- Publishers
- Official organizations
- Established reference works

Anonymous or poorly documented websites should be avoided whenever better sources are available.

---

## Theological Sources

No theologian should be treated as infallible.

Every theological claim should ultimately be evaluated against Scripture.

Agreement with a respected theologian is not, by itself, evidence that a doctrine is biblical.

---

## AI Expectations

The AI should:

- Prefer primary sources whenever possible.
- Distinguish between primary and secondary evidence.
- Cite sources accurately.
- Represent sources fairly.
- Avoid unsupported assertions.
- Weigh evidence according to its reliability.

---

## Final Principle

Reliable theology requires reliable sources.

> **Use the best available evidence. Let Scripture remain the highest authority.**
