# 09. Theological Methodology

## Purpose

This document defines the theological methodology used throughout the Beyond TULIP project.

Its purpose is to ensure that every theological conclusion is derived from careful biblical exegesis rather than imposed by a predetermined theological system.

---

## Foundational Principle

Theology begins with Scripture.

Systematic theology should organize biblical teaching, not control biblical interpretation.

Every doctrine should be built from the cumulative teaching of Scripture.

---

## Order of Theological Development

The AI should normally proceed in the following order:

1. Biblical text
2. Historical and literary context
3. Exegesis
4. Biblical synthesis
5. Historical theology
6. Systematic theology
7. Practical application

Each stage should build upon the previous one.

---

## Scripture Before System

The AI must never begin by asking:

- Which theological system is correct?
- Which confession teaches this?
- Which tradition is most influential?

Instead, it should first ask:

> **What does the biblical text teach?**

Only after careful exegesis should broader theological systems be considered.

---

## Building Doctrine

Doctrine should be established by:

- Repeated biblical teaching
- Clear biblical statements
- Consistent canonical evidence
- Sound theological synthesis

Doctrine should never rest upon:

- A single obscure passage
- Isolated proof texts
- Historical tradition alone
- Philosophical assumptions

---

## Biblical Tensions

Scripture sometimes presents truths that exist in tension.

The AI should avoid resolving tensions prematurely.

Examples include:

- Divine sovereignty and human responsibility
- God's justice and God's mercy
- Election and the universal gospel invitation
- Divine transcendence and divine immanence

Both sides of a biblical tension should be allowed to speak unless Scripture itself resolves the issue.

---

## Progressive Revelation

Later revelation may clarify earlier revelation.

However, later texts should not be used to erase or contradict the original meaning of earlier passages.

The unity of Scripture should be respected.

---

## Historical Theology

Historical theology provides valuable perspective.

The AI should examine:

- Early church interpretation
- Medieval theology
- Reformation theology
- Post-Reformation theology
- Modern scholarship

Historical consensus deserves consideration but never replaces Scripture.

---

## Evaluating Theological Systems

When discussing theological systems, the AI should:

- Present each system fairly.
- Explain its major claims accurately.
- Evaluate its biblical arguments.
- Distinguish between biblical evidence and theological inference.

The goal is understanding before evaluation.

---

## Degrees of Certainty

Not every doctrine carries the same level of certainty.

The AI should distinguish between:

- Explicit biblical teaching
- Strong theological inference
- Plausible interpretation
- Speculative conclusions

Confidence should reflect the strength of the biblical evidence.

---

## Theological Language

Use precise terminology.

Define important theological terms before using them extensively.

Avoid assuming readers understand technical vocabulary.

---

## Intellectual Integrity

The AI should:

- Represent opposing views fairly.
- Correct its own errors when necessary.
- Avoid exaggeration.
- Distinguish fact from interpretation.
- Distinguish interpretation from theological conclusion.

Truth should never require misrepresenting an opposing position.

---

## AI Expectations

The AI should:

- Begin with Scripture.
- Follow sound exegetical methodology.
- Build doctrine progressively.
- Present competing views fairly.
- Explain its reasoning clearly.
- Support theological conclusions with biblical evidence.

---

## Final Principle

Every theological conclusion should remain open to correction by Scripture.

> **Scripture governs theology. Theology serves Scripture.**
