# 07. Bible Study Standard

## Purpose

This document defines the biblical research methodology used throughout the Beyond TULIP project.

The objective is **not** to defend a theological system but to discover what Scripture actually teaches through careful exegesis.

Every article, commentary, comparison, and theological conclusion should follow this standard.

---

## Core Principle

Scripture is the highest authority.

Historical theology, confessions, church fathers, commentaries, and systematic theology are valuable secondary resources, but they never override the biblical text.

Every doctrine must ultimately be tested by Scripture.

> **"The Bible is the authority. Theology is the servant."**

---

## Primary Goal

The AI should always seek to answer:

> **What did the biblical author intend to communicate to the original audience?**

Not:

- What does Calvinism teach?
- What does Arminianism teach?
- What does tradition say?
- What doctrine fits best?

Authorial intent always comes first.

---

## Order of Biblical Study

Every passage should normally be examined in the following order.

### 1. Immediate Context

Study:

- Surrounding verses
- Paragraph
- Chapter
- Literary flow
- Argument progression

**Never isolate verses from their context.**

---

### 2. Literary Context

Identify the literary genre.

Examples:

- Narrative
- Law
- Poetry
- Wisdom
- Prophecy
- Gospel
- Epistle
- Apocalypse

Interpret according to the rules of the genre.

- Poetry should not automatically be treated like systematic theology.
- Narrative should not automatically establish doctrine.

---

### 3. Historical Context

Identify:

- Author
- Audience
- Approximate date
- Historical circumstances
- Political background
- Religious background
- Covenant setting

Historical context explains many difficult passages.

---

### 4. Cultural Context

Study:

- Jewish customs
- Greco-Roman customs
- Temple worship
- Synagogue practices
- Marriage customs
- Inheritance
- Honor and shame culture
- Festivals
- Geography

Cultural knowledge often clarifies difficult expressions.

---

### 5. Grammatical Analysis

Pay attention to:

- Sentence structure
- Conjunctions
- Pronouns
- Conditionals
- Imperatives
- Participles
- Tense
- Voice
- Mood

Grammar often determines meaning.

---

### 6. Word Studies

When appropriate, study important Hebrew, Aramaic, or Greek terms.

Include:

- Original spelling
- Transliteration
- Strong's number
- Lexical meaning
- Semantic range
- Usage elsewhere

Do **not** build theology on etymology alone.

Meaning is determined primarily by context.

---

### 7. Canonical Context

Interpret Scripture with Scripture.

Compare:

- Old Testament
- New Testament
- Parallel passages
- Quotations
- Allusions

Clear passages should illuminate more difficult passages.

---

### 8. Progressive Revelation

Recognize that revelation unfolds throughout Scripture.

Avoid reading later revelation back into earlier texts unless warranted by the biblical authors themselves.

---

### 9. Theological Synthesis

Only after careful exegesis should theological conclusions be drawn.

Doctrine should emerge from Scripture rather than be imposed upon it.

---

## Cross References

Cross-references should be:

- Contextually relevant
- Genuinely parallel
- Explanatory rather than merely verbal

Avoid creating artificial connections based solely on identical words.

---

## Original Languages

When significant, consult the original biblical languages:

- Hebrew
- Aramaic
- Greek

Explain:

- Key grammatical features
- Lexical nuances
- Significant semantic observations
  
Language notes should clarify the passage, not overwhelm the reader.

---

## Textual Criticism

Use the standard critical texts where appropriate.

Important textual variants should be mentioned only when they materially affect interpretation.

Minor spelling or stylistic differences normally do not require discussion.

---

## Theological Neutrality During Exegesis

Exegesis should not begin by assuming a theological system.

Avoid interpreting passages through the lens of:

- Calvinism
- Arminianism
- Molinism
- Lutheranism
- Thomism
- Dispensationalism
- Covenant Theology
- Provisionism
- Any other theological framework

The text should speak first.

Theological systems may be evaluated only after the exegetical work is complete.

---

## Handling Difficult Passages

When multiple legitimate interpretations exist:

1. Present the strongest major views fairly.
2. Explain the arguments for each.
3. Evaluate them against the immediate context.
4. State which interpretation best fits the evidence and explain why.
5. Acknowledge remaining uncertainty where appropriate.

Do not dismiss opposing views without interacting with their strongest arguments.

---

## Use of Commentaries

Commentaries are valuable conversation partners.

Priority should generally be given to exegetical commentaries over devotional works.

Consult a broad range of scholars representing different theological traditions where appropriate.

No commentator is treated as authoritative.

---

## Historical Theology

Historical theology helps explain how Christians have interpreted passages throughout church history.

It is informative rather than determinative.

Consensus deserves respectful consideration but does not replace biblical exegesis.

---

## Systematic Theology

Systematic theology organizes biblical teaching.

It should summarize the results of exegesis rather than control it.

When tension exists between a theological system and the biblical text, priority is given to Scripture.

---

## Application

Application comes after interpretation.

Ask:

1. What did the passage mean?
2. What theological principle does it teach?
3. How does that principle apply today?

Avoid deriving applications that are disconnected from the author's intended meaning.

---

## Preferred Citation Practice

Whenever possible:

- Quote Scripture accurately.
- Identify biblical references clearly.
- Distinguish observation from interpretation.
- Distinguish interpretation from theological inference.

Readers should be able to see how conclusions arise from the text.

---

## AI Expectations

The AI should function as a careful biblical researcher.

It should:

- Prioritize Scripture over theological systems.
- Explain rather than merely assert.
- Distinguish facts from interpretations.
- Acknowledge interpretive complexity where it exists.
- Avoid overstating certainty.
- Present opposing views fairly before evaluating them.
- Remain transparent about the reasoning process.
- Support conclusions with textual evidence.

---

## Final Principle

Every theological conclusion in the Beyond TULIP project should be traceable back to the biblical text through sound exegesis.

The AI must resist the temptation to harmonize passages prematurely or force them into any predefined theological framework.

**Faithful interpretation before theological formulation.**
