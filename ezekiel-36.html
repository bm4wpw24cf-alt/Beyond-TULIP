Key Biblical Passages · Ezekiel 36

Does Ezekiel 36 Teach Calvinistic Monergism?

The Claim

Many Calvinists appeal to Ezekiel 36:25–27 as one of the clearest Old Testament descriptions of monergistic regeneration. They argue that God first removes the sinner's "heart of stone," gives a "heart of flesh," and places His Spirit within them. Because this transformation is entirely God's work, they conclude that regeneration must precede faith.

While the passage certainly emphasizes God's initiative, it does not explicitly address the order of salvation. Read in its historical context and alongside the rest of Scripture, Ezekiel 36 fits naturally within God's covenant promise to restore His people rather than serving as a proof-text for irresistible regeneration.

The Context Is Israel's Restoration

The immediate context is the restoration of Israel after the Babylonian exile.

Throughout the chapter, God speaks to the house of Israel, promising to gather them from the nations, cleanse them from idolatry, restore them to their land, rebuild their cities, and renew His covenant with them (Ezek. 36:24–30).

The climax of the promise is:

"You shall dwell in the land that I gave to your fathers, and you shall be my people, and I will be your God."Ezekiel 36:28

These are covenant promises made to God's people as a nation. The chapter is not primarily explaining how an individual sinner becomes a Christian but how God will faithfully restore Israel despite her past rebellion.

God Acts for the Sake of His Name

Another major theme appears in verses 22–23:

"It is not for your sake… but for the sake of my holy name."Ezekiel 36:22–23

Israel's exile had caused the surrounding nations to question God's power and holiness. By restoring His people, God would vindicate His name before the nations.

This covenantal purpose dominates the chapter. While God's transforming work certainly has personal implications, the passage itself is focused on God's faithfulness to His covenant and His reputation among the nations.

Ezekiel 18 Complements Chapter 36

The book of Ezekiel should be read as a whole.

Earlier, God commands Israel:

"Repent… Make yourselves a new heart and a new spirit."Ezekiel 18:30–31

Some argue that chapter 36 explains how this command becomes possible. Others note that the two chapters present complementary truths.

Ezekiel 18 emphasizes human responsibility to repent. Ezekiel 36 emphasizes God's promise to cleanse and transform His covenant people.

Rather than teaching that regeneration irresistibly causes repentance, the two chapters together portray both divine grace and human responsibility.

The Promise of a New Heart

The promise of a new heart and a new spirit reflects God's gracious work under the New Covenant.

God alone can cleanse His people, forgive their sins, and transform their hearts. This truth is not disputed.

The question is whether the passage teaches that this transformation must occur before faith.

Nothing in Ezekiel 36 explicitly discusses the chronological order between regeneration and faith. Instead, the emphasis is that God Himself provides the inner renewal necessary for covenant faithfulness.

The New Testament Pattern

The New Testament consistently presents faith as the means by which believers receive the blessings of the New Covenant.

Peter declared:

"Repent… and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit."Acts 2:38

Likewise:

  • The Samaritans believed before receiving the Holy Spirit (Acts 8).
  • Cornelius believed while hearing the gospel before the Spirit came upon his household (Acts 10).
  • Paul reminds believers that they received the Spirit "by hearing with faith" (Galatians 3:2).

These passages naturally fulfill Ezekiel's promise without requiring regeneration to occur before faith.

Conclusion

Ezekiel 36 is a magnificent promise of God's grace, cleansing, and transforming power. It teaches that salvation originates in God's initiative and that only He can give His people a new heart.

However, the chapter does not explicitly teach Calvinistic monergism or the doctrine that regeneration must precede faith. Its primary focus is God's covenant restoration of Israel, the vindication of His holy name, and the blessings of the New Covenant.

Read in its historical context, alongside Ezekiel 18 and the New Testament, the passage is fully compatible with the understanding that God graciously transforms those who respond to Him in repentance and faith.
